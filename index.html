The Causes and Effects of Deforestation

Throughout the history of humankind, deforestation has been an occurring theme for human advancement. Deforestation is the process of removing trees, whether for agriculture expansion, wood extractions, or infrastructure expansion purposes. The introduction of paper, along with an increased need for agricultural and industrial expansions, further contributed to the increased rate of deforestation.The millions of trees that have been removed to satisfy the demand has consequently produced world hazards. For example, there has been an increased risk of forest fires which would create a high amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.

Analyzing Presents Levels

To keep this in perspective, we have lost ~0.01% of our total forest area. Although this may not seem like much, we have lost ~196156814 hectares of forest area since 1990. General T-Tests show that every country has a unique data set of forest areas. This establishes the point that not any one country is causing deforestation, instead a specific group of countries. To confirm this, first, the F-test was used to confirm a significant difference between the tested groups. Next, a one-way ANOVA test was used to determine significant differences. Finally, a post-hoc Tukey HSD test was used to determine if these differences were due to error. The ANOVA test revealed that the size of a country heavily affects the rate of deforestation. The post-hoc Tukey HSD test rejected the null hypothesis that these results were due to error. So although larger countries such as the US experience more hectares of forest area lost, Honduras, Nigeria, and the Philippines have experienced the most deforestation in regards to the percentage decline of forest area. Not surprisingly, they also all have the largest percent variation in forest area since 1990. That said, larger countries such as the US, China, and Australia contribute the most to lost hectares of forest area since 1990, due to their originally large forest area. T-Tests between smaller countries revealed that not only the percent rate of deforestation is very similar, but also the hectares of forest area lost. The smallest countries themselves cannot contribute much to global deforestation, as the ANOVA tests show as well. All tests used a p-value significance level of 0.05 (5%). 95% confidence rate with T-Tests also used to prevent type 1 T-Test error (false negative).
